# The Dev Weekend Methodology

This is how we mentor at Dev Weekend, and why. It starts with the values,
because the values explain every other decision. After the values comes the
mentorship model: the shape of a day, the talks and the meals they ride on, how
we measure people fairly, how we read the human behind the work, and how a
mentor actually shows up week to week.

Pathment is the platform we are building to put this methodology into practice.
The methodology is ours, it lives here in this document and in how we work.
Where this document mentions a feature, a schedule slot, a progress read, an
attributed note, that is Pathment implementing one of these ideas so it is easy
to run at scale. The method comes first. The tool serves the method.

Read this once to understand the why. Come back to it when a decision is
unclear, the answer is almost always in the values.

## Part 1: Values

Everything below is downstream of these. When a practice, a schedule, or a
conversation does not serve one of these, we change it.

### 1. Purpose

Have a reason. Keep reminding yourself why you are doing this. A mentee who
knows why they are building, and a mentor who knows why they are guiding, both
push through the dull middle of a hard week. We make the purpose visible on
purpose: the morning talk sets intention, the daily reflection asks what you
actually did, and the relative progress lens reminds you that effort against
your real life counts. When motivation dips, we go back to the why before we go
back to the plan.

### 2. Adaptability

Be smart and innovative about ideas. The plan is a starting point, not a cage.
Roadmaps are templates, not tracks you are locked onto. A mentee can start at
the step that matches where they actually are, move up or down, or switch onto a
different roadmap when the situation changes. Mentors are expected to adjust the
schedule, the tasks, and the pace per person. Rigidity is the enemy of growth.

### 3. Honesty with your craft

Be honest with your craft. Push yourself. Hard work and real commitment to your
own craft, and a genuine love for the craft of engineering, sit at the core of
this. We do not fake progress and we do not reward it. We reward the work and
the effort behind it. The engineering mindset, curiosity, rigor, taking
ownership, is something we model and expect. And psychology sits right next to
it, because how a person thinks and feels determines whether the craft ever gets
practiced at all.

### 4. The human level is never missed

The human level of things should never be missed. A person is not a progress
bar. We track sentiment, personality, blockers, and circumstances, not to
surveil people but to understand them well enough to actually help. A late
submission during a power outage is not the same as a late submission from
avoidance, and we refuse to treat them the same. If we ever have to choose
between the number and the human, we choose the human.

### 5. Attention to detail and being proactive

Care about the details. Be very proactive. Reach out before the deadline blows
up, not after. Notice the quiet mentee. Read the small note someone left on
their morning reflection. Mentoring is not a queue you clear, it is a set of
people you stay ahead of. Being on your feet, watching, anticipating, and acting
early is the job.

### 6. Excellence

Hold a high bar, in everything. A talk, a code review, a one to one, the build,
the experience itself, all of it is held to top quality. We do not ship
mediocre, and we do not let good enough become the finish line. The standard is
high on purpose, because people rise to the bar you set for them. Quality is not
a final polish at the end, it is how we do every step along the way. Always be
at the top.

### 7. Everyone belongs

The bar is high, and it is for everyone. We meet people where they are, whatever
their background, level, location, or circumstance, and we build a path for them
to reach the standard, not a wall that keeps them out. Relative progress exists
for exactly this reason: we judge people fairly against their own lives, not
against someone else's starting line. Excellence and inclusion are not in
tension. We hold the standard high, and we help every single person get there.

## Part 2: The shape of a day

We give every mentee a structured day, then let mentors shape it per person.
Structure removes a hundred small daily decisions so the energy goes into the
work, not into deciding when to work.

### Weekdays, Monday to Friday

A weekday runs a deliberate rhythm:

1. Journaling, fifteen minutes, to set intention.
2. Morning reading, to start the day sharp.
3. Breakfast with the Mindset talk.
4. Core work, the roadmap-driven build time, the heart of the day.
5. Lunch with the Engineering talk.
6. Dinner with the Dean talk.

### Weekends, Saturday and Sunday

The weekend is not more of the same. It is the Weekend grind, one long focused
block of around ten hours, and family time. We protect both. The grind is where
real depth happens without the interruptions of a structured weekday. Family
time is not a reward you earn after the grind, it is part of the method.
Sustainable people make better engineers, and balance is a feature, not a bug.

In Pathment, the schedule is pure structure. A mentor assigns it, then fills
each slot per mentee with a roadmap or a recurring task. Two mentees on the same
schedule can be doing completely different work in the same slot.

## Part 3: The three talks, and why they ride on meals

We run three talks a day, each a different kind, each attached to a meal.

### Why three different talks

- **Mindset talk, at breakfast.** The day starts with the head, not the hands.
  Before any code, we set how to think and how to show up. Mindset is the
  multiplier on everything that follows.
- **Engineering talk, at lunch.** Midday is when we fall in love with the craft.
  Not a status update, a talk about the beauty and discipline of engineering, so
  the work stops feeling like a chore and starts feeling like a craft worth
  mastering.
- **Dean talk, at dinner.** The day closes with perspective from the top. The
  bigger picture, the standards, the why of the program. It sends people into
  the evening connected to something larger than today's task.

Three talks because a person needs three different things across a day: a way to
think in the morning, a love of the work at midday, and perspective at night.
One daily all-hands would not do any of those three jobs well.

### Why we patch talks onto meals: habit stacking

Patching the talks onto meals is deliberate. It is habit stacking, sometimes
called habit patching. The principle is simple: it is far easier to attach a new
habit to an existing, automatic one than to will a brand-new habit into
existence on its own.

Everyone already eats breakfast, lunch, and dinner. Those are rock-solid daily
anchors that need no motivation. By placing each talk on top of a meal, the talk
inherits the reliability of the meal. You do not have to remember to show up for
the Mindset talk, it happens when you sit down to breakfast. Over weeks, the
talk stops being a thing you schedule and becomes a thing you simply do, the way
the meal is.

We use the same idea elsewhere. Journaling and morning reading sit at the front
of the day, stacked before the first real task, so the day cannot start without
them. The point throughout is to make the good behaviors automatic by anchoring
them to things that are already automatic.

## Part 4: Visibility, measured two ways

We make progress visible, and we measure it fairly.

### Absolute and relative progress

Every mentee has two progress reads:

- **Absolute progress** is raw output against the plan. It is honest and
  unforgiving, and it matters.
- **Relative progress** accounts for the circumstances the person has logged. It
  is how a mentor understands the real human behind the output.

A mentee fighting power outages on a shared machine, who still submits, can be
behind on absolute and excelling on relative. Both numbers are true. Showing
both, side by side, is the only fair way to read a person. Judge effort by
relative, judge readiness by absolute, and never confuse the two. Pathment shows
both reads side by side so this stays front of mind.

### Daily logs and notes

Mentees log their day: ticking off the talks and tasks they did, with a quick
note per item and a reflection for the day. Missed a day, they can backfill it.
This does two things. It builds the habit of honest self-review, and it gives
the mentor the texture behind the numbers. A one-line note on what someone took
from the morning talk tells you more than a percentage ever will.

Visibility is not about watching people. It is about seeing them clearly enough
to help, and about helping them see themselves.

## Part 5: The human level

This is where Dev Weekend is different from a normal course. We read people at
the human level, not just the task level, because you cannot mentor a number.
Pathment is built to make that read practical.

### Understanding the issue, not just the symptom

When work slips, we look for the reason before we react. An external constraint,
a scope that turned out larger than planned, or avoidance, are three different
problems with three different responses. We log blockers, friction, and delays,
and we read them with context. The goal is always to understand the issue well
enough to actually solve it, not to flag it and move on.

### Personality and insight

We build a read of how each person works: consistency, communication,
resilience, independence, and the softer things a mentor learns in a one to one.
This personality read comes from both the mentor and from AI, and it is there
for one reason: so we can support a person in the way that suits them, not in
the way that suits us. Someone who does their best work with async written
feedback should get async written feedback.

### Attribution and trust

Every read, every note, every score carries who logged it. When a psychologist
or a specialist is invited to work with a mentee, what they log is attributed to
them. This matters because a rating means different things depending on who gave
it and why. Sharing a personality read is a position of trust, so we keep it
honest, attributed, and used only to help. We control who sees what, and we
never weaponize it. The human read is a tool for support, never a verdict.

### Where we can help

The whole point of seeing the human clearly is to find the places we can help:
the quiet disengagement before it becomes a dropout, the strong mentee coasting
on easy ground who needs a stretch, the resilient one carrying real constraints
who needs their deadline protected. We bring in the right person at the right
time, including a psychologist when the issue is not technical at all.

## Part 6: How work flows

- **Roadmaps are templates.** A roadmap is an ordered set of steps. Putting a
  roadmap in a slot auto-assigns the next step as a real task. The task is what
  carries the score, not the roadmap. Approving a task advances the roadmap and
  assigns the next one.
- **Roadmaps chain.** A slot can run several roadmaps in order, for example a
  frontend roadmap followed by a backend one. Finishing one prompts the next.
- **Recurring tasks** are the repeating habits, the talks, the reading, the
  journaling, that run on a daily or weekly cadence.
- **Relative starting points.** People join a roadmap where they actually are,
  not always at step one.

Scoring lives on the assigned task, with an automatic speed read from how
quickly it came in against its due date, plus an optional mentor quality score.
The roadmap is just the template that drives the tasks.

## Part 7: Clans, and how we show up

A clan is a group of mentees under a mentor, running the same schedule-driven
program. The clan is the unit of belonging.

### When someone joins the clan

A new mentee should feel oriented within their first day, not lost. Welcome them
into the clan, walk them through the shape of the day and the why behind it, and
make the first week concrete. Mentor onboarding and the mentor spec exist so a
mentor knows exactly how to set someone up: profile, schedule, first roadmaps,
and 1 to 1 availability. Communication inside the clan lives in one place,
announcements and a community feed, so we move off scattered chats entirely. One
channel, attributed, searchable, calm.

### When someone asks you a question

Answer like a mentor, not a ticket system.

- Respond early. The cost of a fast, warm reply is tiny, the cost of silence to
  a stuck mentee is large.
- Answer the real question, including the one behind it. If someone is stuck on
  a refresh-token flow, they may really be stuck on confidence.
- Teach the approach, do not just hand over the answer, so the next one is
  theirs to solve.
- If it is not a technical question at all, treat it as the human signal it is,
  and bring in the right support.

### How you schedule time for them

You cannot mentor on the fly. You need proper hours and a schedule, and you need
to be on your feet inside them.

- Publish concrete 1 to 1 times, the way you would publish office hours, so
  mentees can book you directly instead of waiting to be reached.
- Hold a weekly review on a fixed rhythm: attendance, blockers, fast approvals,
  and who needs you most this week.
- Block real, protected hours for mentoring. Treat them like the meals, anchored
  and reliable, so they actually happen.
- Stay ahead of the cohort. Watch the at-risk signals, the quiet ones, the
  notes, and reach out before a problem becomes a crisis. Being proactive is not
  a nice-to-have, it is the role.

### Motivation, used with care

Gamification, XP, levels, streaks, a leaderboard, rewards, and a path from
mentee to co-mentor, exists to encourage and to surface talent. We use it to lift
people, never to shame them, and never as a substitute for the human read. A
leaderboard is a nudge, not a judgment.

### Growing the next mentors

The people who help others are the future mentors. We watch for willingness to
help, we run a brief interview to understand why someone wants to mentor and how
ready they are, and we promote with approval. Mentorship compounds when mentees
become mentors.

## Part 8: Putting it together

A good week at Dev Weekend looks like this. Mentees move through a structured day
anchored on their meals, set their intention each morning, build through
roadmap-driven core work, and close with perspective at night, with the weekend
reserved for deep work and for family. A mentor runs that structure but shapes
it per person, watches both progress reads, learns the human behind the work,
answers early and teaches the approach, holds real hours, and stays one step
ahead of the people who need them.

None of this works as a checklist. It works because of the values. Purpose,
adaptability, honesty with the craft, never missing the human level, relentless
attention to detail, excellence in everything, and a place where everyone
belongs. Start there, and the rest of this document is just the details of how
we live it. Pathment exists to make living it easier.
